# Wiring for Impact

*15 questions · ~5 minutes · Single choice · Covers 5 layers: pressure, contribution, energy, leadership, decision-making*


## Section A — How You Show Up Under Pressure


### Q1 — Crisis scenario

> Your team just discovered a critical error 2 hours before a major deadline. What do you do first?

- **A.** Stop and diagnose exactly what went wrong before touching anything → Realist
- **B.** Immediately start fixing the most visible problem → Doer
- **C.** Gather the team, assign roles, and create a rapid recovery plan → Organizer
- **D.** Keep everyone's energy up — panic is the biggest threat right now → Encourager
- **E.** Step back and decide whether the deadline itself needs renegotiation → Wise Observer

### Q2 — Ambiguity scenario

> You're handed a project with no clear brief, a tight timeline, and a team that's never worked together. What's your first move?

- **A.** Map out everything that's unclear and resolve the ambiguity before starting → Realist/Explainer
- **B.** Start doing the most obvious work immediately — clarity comes through action → Doer
- **C.** Build a shared structure: roles, milestones, communication norms → Organizer
- **D.** Get the team energized around a shared purpose before any logistics → Encourager
- **E.** Identify who on the team is most capable and organize around their strengths → Wise Observer

## Section B — How You Contribute to Teams


### Q3 — Meeting scenario

> In a meeting that's going in circles, what do you find yourself doing?

- **A.** Pointing out the logical flaw or gap that's causing the loop → Realist
- **B.** Suggesting the group just pick something and move — progress beats perfection → Doer
- **C.** Summarizing what's been said and proposing a clear decision framework → Organizer
- **D.** Checking in on how people are feeling — tension is usually the real blocker → Supporter
- **E.** Asking a question that reframes the problem entirely → Wise Observer

### Q4 — Colleague support scenario

> A colleague is struggling and falling behind. You have 30 minutes to help. What do you do?

- **A.** Sit with them and think through the root cause of why they're stuck → Realist/Explainer
- **B.** Take some tasks off their plate and handle them yourself → Doer
- **C.** Help them reorganize and prioritize so the work feels manageable → Organizer
- **D.** Just be present with them — they need support more than solutions → Supporter
- **E.** Coach them through it so they can handle it themselves going forward → Encourager

## Section C — What Energizes You


### Q5 — Free time scenario

> It's a Friday afternoon and you have a free block of time. What kind of work would feel most satisfying?

- **A.** Deeply analyzing a complex problem that's been bothering me → Realist/Explainer
- **B.** Crossing things off a list and getting ahead on execution → Doer
- **C.** Building a system, framework, or process for something messy → Organizer
- **D.** Mentoring someone or having a meaningful conversation → Supporter/Encourager
- **E.** Exploring a new idea or possibility without any pressure to deliver → Wise Observer

### Q6 — New responsibility scenario

> You've just been given a significant new responsibility. What's your honest internal reaction?

- **A.** I want to understand exactly what success looks like before I commit → Realist
- **B.** I'm ready — let's figure it out as we go → Doer
- **C.** I start thinking about how to organize everything to make it work → Organizer
- **D.** I think about who I need to bring with me and how to keep them supported → Supporter
- **E.** I feel energized by the opportunity to do something meaningful → Enterpriser

## Section D — How You Lead


### Q7 — Process defiance scenario

> You're leading a project and a team member keeps doing things their own way, ignoring the agreed process. What do you do?

- **A.** Have a direct conversation about why the standard exists and what's at risk → Realist
- **B.** Give them space — if their output is good, the process matters less → Wise Observer
- **C.** Revisit the process with the whole team to see if it actually needs updating → Organizer
- **D.** Try to understand what's driving their behavior before addressing it → Supporter
- **E.** Clarify the expectation one-on-one and reset the agreement clearly → Explainer

### Q8 — Team setback scenario

> Your team is discouraged after a setback. What do you do?

- **A.** Show them exactly what went wrong and how to correct it — clarity is reassurance → Realist/Explainer
- **B.** Acknowledge the loss, but get everyone back into motion quickly → Doer
- **C.** Help them see where they still are in the journey and what comes next → Organizer
- **D.** Make space for people to process — rushing past it makes it worse → Supporter
- **E.** Reframe the setback as information and redirect energy toward what's possible → Encourager/Wise Observer

## Section E — How You Make Decisions & Resilience


### Q9 — Time-pressured decision

> You have to make a significant decision with incomplete information and a 24-hour deadline. What guides you?

- **A.** I gather as much data as possible and use logic to find the best option → Realist/Explainer
- **B.** I talk it through with one or two people I trust → Supporter
- **C.** I assess practical implications: what's reversible, what isn't → Doer
- **D.** I evaluate which direction feels most aligned with long-term purpose → Wise Observer
- **E.** I follow my gut — I've usually already processed it subconsciously → Enterpriser

### Q10 — Career choice scenario

> You're evaluating two career opportunities. One is stable and clear. The other is risky but deeply aligned with your purpose. What matters most in your decision?

- **A.** Whether I have the capability and track record to succeed in the risky one → Realist
- **B.** Which one I can actually execute and make work right now → Doer
- **C.** Which one positions me better for the long term → Wise Observer/Organizer
- **D.** Which one will let me be most present for the people who matter to me → Supporter
- **E.** Which one I'll regret not taking → Enterpriser

### Q11 — Plan failure scenario

> A plan you built carefully is falling apart. What do you do?

- **A.** Stop and diagnose: find the root cause before changing anything → Realist
- **B.** Adapt in real time — I'll fix it as new information comes in → Doer
- **C.** Rebuild the structure: a new plan that accounts for what we now know → Organizer
- **D.** Check in on the people involved — their capacity matters more than the plan right now → Supporter
- **E.** Step back entirely and ask whether the goal itself is still right → Wise Observer

### Q12 — Public challenge scenario

> Someone challenges your idea publicly in a meeting. What's your instinct?

- **A.** Defend it with data and logic — if it's right, I should be able to prove it → Realist
- **B.** Welcome the pushback — challenge usually strengthens the idea → Doer/Encourager
- **C.** Ask them to be specific: what exactly is the objection? → Explainer
- **D.** Notice how others in the room are reacting before I respond → Supporter/Wise Observer
- **E.** Use their objection to reframe the idea in a way that addresses it → Integrator

### Q13 — Failure analysis scenario

> You read a fascinating article about a company that failed despite having a great product. What question stays with you?

- **A.** What was the underlying flaw in the business model or strategy? → Realist
- **B.** Who were the people involved and what drove their decisions? → Supporter
- **C.** What could they have done differently at each decision point? → Doer/Explainer
- **D.** What systems or structures broke down that a better-run organization would have caught? → Organizer
- **E.** What does this tell us about what actually makes something succeed? → Wise Observer

### Q14 — Natural role reflection

> Looking back on the projects where you felt most alive and useful, what role were you usually playing?

- **A.** The one who caught problems others missed and kept things honest → Realist
- **B.** The one who kept things moving when momentum stalled → Doer
- **C.** The one who brought structure and clarity to what was chaotic → Organizer
- **D.** The one who kept the people healthy, supported, and moving together → Supporter
- **E.** The one who reframed the problem and opened up new possibilities → Wise Observer

### Q15 — Superpower scenario

> If your team had to describe your superpower in one sentence, which would ring most true?

- **A.** They see what's actually going on beneath the surface → Realist
- **B.** They make things happen — they turn talk into action → Doer
- **C.** They bring order — chaos becomes a plan when they're involved → Organizer
- **D.** They hold the team together — people feel safe and supported → Supporter
- **E.** They see possibilities before anyone else does → Wise Observer/Enterpriser

## Section F — Compressed Mode Detection (added)

These four questions are added at the end of the existing fifteen Wiring for Impact questions. The section label shown to users is: "One more set — these go a little deeper." The internal label for developers is Section F: Compressed Mode Detection.


### Q16

*Section F · Compressed Mode Detection*

> When you have given everything to a situation and the pressure still has not let up — when patience is at its limit — what do you honestly tend to do?

- **A.** I take over. I stop asking and start directing. If it needs to be done, I will do it myself.
- **B.** I go quiet. I stop engaging and let things run without me for a while.   →  Compressed pattern: Strategic withdrawal — high Extroversion turning inward
- **C.** I become exact. I insist on doing things the right way even if that slows everything down.   →  Compressed pattern: Precision lock — high Conformity under depletion
- **D.** I go through the motions. I show up and complete tasks but I have mentally separated from the outcome.   →  Compressed pattern: Disconnected execution — high Doer turning mechanical
- **E.** I become more candid than useful. I find it hard to stay quiet about what is wrong, regardless of timing.   →  Compressed pattern: Unfiltered critique — high Realist gift losing restraint

> **Dev note:  Store as COMPRESSED_MODE_PRIMARY. Cross-reference with Q17 for activation condition. If Q19 matches Q16, compressed profile confidence = HIGH. If mismatch, store both and flag TENTATIVE.**


### Q17

*Section F · Compressed Mode Detection*

> Think of a time you were genuinely operating at your least effective. What was the condition that brought you there?

- **A.** Incompetence or avoidable errors I could not correct fast enough.   →  Activation condition: Quality failure — Organizer / Realist profile
- **B.** Being dismissed, overridden, or not taken seriously.   →  Activation condition: Disrespect — high Dominance / Extroversion profile
- **C.** Being pushed to move faster than my standard of care allows.   →  Activation condition: Accuracy compromise — high Conformity profile
- **D.** Carrying responsibility for an outcome I had no real control over.   →  Activation condition: Accountability without authority
- **E.** An environment with no momentum — slow, repetitive, or without purpose.   →  Activation condition: Environment mismatch — high Doer / Pace profile

> **Dev note:  Store as COMPRESSED_ACTIVATION_CONDITION. AI uses this variable to predict when compressed behavior is likely — not only to describe it after the fact. Cross-match against B3 Environment domain score.**


### Q18

*Section F · Compressed Mode Detection*

> After you have moved through a difficult period and come back to yourself — what does recovery typically look like for you?

- **A.** I process internally first. I need to understand what happened before I can talk about it or move.   →  Recovery mode: Internal processor — needs space before repair
- **B.** I address it directly. I go back to the situation or person and say what I need to say.   →  Recovery mode: Direct repairer
- **C.** I give it time. I let things settle without forcing a conversation or resolution.   →  Recovery mode: Time-based restoration
- **D.** I adjust the structure. I identify the root condition and fix it so the same situation does not recur.   →  Recovery mode: Structural repairer
- **E.** I get back into motion. Completing something, producing something — action is how I reset.   →  Recovery mode: Motion-based reset

> **Dev note:  Store as COMPRESSED_RECOVERY_MODE. AI uses this to know how to respond when a user signals depletion. Do not offer forward-planning or action steps before offering the recovery-appropriate framing.**


### Q19

*Section F · Compressed Mode Detection*

> Looking back honestly at the version of you that shows up under those conditions — what do others typically experience in that moment?

- **A.** Someone who takes charge forcefully and stops listening.   →  Self-awareness: High — compressed pattern is known and observable to self
- **B.** Someone who becomes cold or emotionally unavailable.   →  Self-awareness: Moderate — aware of withdrawal pattern
- **C.** Someone who becomes harder to satisfy and slower to trust.   →  Self-awareness: Moderate — aware of precision / critique pattern
- **D.** Someone who stays present physically but stops contributing meaningfully.   →  Self-awareness: Partial — aware of disconnection
- **E.** I honestly do not know — I am usually not aware of how I come across in those moments.   →  Self-awareness: Low — set COMPRESSED_BLIND_SPOT = TRUE

> **Dev note:  Store as COMPRESSED_SELF_AWARENESS. If answer is E, set COMPRESSED_BLIND_SPOT = TRUE. When this flag is active, AI offers behavioral signals the user can observe in others' responses — not the compressed mode label directly. The label is only shared after the user asks explicitly or after trust has been established across multiple sessions.**


## Section 5.1.1 — Non-negotiable rules

Compressed mode data must never appear in any user-facing result, score, or recommendation without being framed in conditional, predictive language. It must never reduce a user's assessed capability, opportunity alignment score, or any other output that carries evaluative weight. It exists to expand awareness and enable protective guidance only.
